01 — Context

Misplaced trolleys are a cascade problem.

Abandoned trolleys cost retailers money, create safety hazards, and frustrate shoppers daily. The behaviour persists because the return process is unrewarding and easy to ignore — a human-behaviour problem as much as a systemic one.

“I almost always put my trolley back, but I think it should be okay not to sometimes — there are people who do it as their job.”

02 — The move

Make returning the trolley its own reward.

Research pointed to two levers: incentive and visibility. So rather than nag shoppers, we redesigned the return point itself into a “Smart Station” — a main display, an interactive tablet, and a trolley-sensing frame that together turn a return into a small, satisfying win.

Testing then reshaped three decisions that made or broke the experience: the reward QR moved to the personal tablet for privacy, a jarring flicker became a smooth rolling gradient, and a minigame bottleneck was cut for a timer to keep the flow quick.

03 — Outcome

A return that feels like a reward.

The final hi-fi system turns a chore into a rewarding interaction — clear feedback, a “Wheel of Prize” moment on return, and a private reward delivered to the user’s own screen.

Next steps: A/B test the messaging and reward system, then run a live pilot to validate efficiency and adoption in a real retail environment.

Research & discovery +

Surveys, interviews, and observation showed trolley misplacement is driven by a mix of systemic flaws and human behaviour:

💡

Poor User Experience

1

The return process lacks clear instructions, making it confusing.

💰

Lack of Incentives

2

Little motivation to return trolleys, especially in a hurry.

🛒

Bandwagon Effect

3

People abandon trolleys when they see others do the same.

😒

“Not My Issue”

4

Many feel collection is the store’s job, not theirs.

Process & iterations +

A persona (Josh, 26) and journey map grounded the design; usability testing surfaced the issues that drove the final iteration.

🔒 Privacy

Vouchers on the public screen raised privacy concerns → moved QR to the personal tablet.

🖼️ Clarity

A flickering display added cognitive load → replaced with a smooth rolling gradient.

⏩ Flow

A minigame bottlenecked the journey → removed for a timer to streamline and prevent abuse.
